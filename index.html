Recycle Garbage and Waste for Economic and Environmental Purposes

Overview Causes Impacts

Egypt faces a monumental waste management crisis, with over 95 million tons of municipal solid waste (MSW) generated annually. A large population exceeding 105 million and rapid urbanization (43% population growth since 2000), and limited landfill capacity have transformed waste recovery into a strategic imperative; despite this, recycling rates are below 20%. It is a very low percentage for a civilized country like Egypt. Recycling is the process of taking materials that would normally be thrown away (waste) and turning them into new, useful products. Egypt ranks 8th in the world in 2019 for mismanaged plastic waste with 1,435,510 ton, according to figure 8 Egypt is the biggest contributor to plastic pollution in the Mediterranean Sea with rate of 74,000 tons/year (42% of total plastic waste in the Mediterranean) it is not a low rate as the Mediterranean Sea holds only 1% of the world's waters and 7% of global plastic wastes with rate of 730 tons/day. Egyptian people generate 21K tons/day of municipal solid waste (Cairo alone: 15K tons/day).

Economically, waste represents a lost resource. Recycling plastics, metals, paper, and organic waste can generate billions of Egyptian pounds annually, create thousands of jobs, and support small and medium enterprises.

Causes

1.Weak Formal Recycling Systems and Policy Implementation:

The development of formal recycling systems and policy enforcement speed represents a fundamental reason for this problem. The formal recycling rate for municipal solid waste in Egypt reached 15.9% during 2021. The government established a goal to achieve 60% recycling rates for municipal waste by 2027. The number of recycling facilities has shown growth, but remains lacking, with 28 facilities operating and plans to reach 56 facilities. The data indicate that improvement efforts are in progress, but the existing formal waste management systems fail to handle the current waste volume. The current waste management system operates at a lacking capacity to handle the large amount of generated materials.

2.Rapid Waste Generation and Collection Gaps:

The large amount of municipal solid waste with not enough waste collection systems represents a reason for Egypt's waste management problems. As shown in Figure 9, the 2021 municipal solid waste (MSW) production in Egypt reached 21.2 million tons, which can be estimated to be 251 kilograms of waste per person annually, while the recycled MSW was 15.9% from the total MSW, which is approximately 3.37 million tons. The daily waste output in Cairo reached 17,850 tons during 2021. The waste management system faces intense pressure because population expansion and urban development produce much waste, but the system lacks collection and recycling capabilities. The combination of waste production and bad waste management systems leads to increased landfill usage, environmental dangers, and operational problems. The current waste management system fails to recover recyclable materials at their origin, which results in resource waste and higher waste management expenses.

3.Economic Dependence on the Informal Waste Sector and limited space for them:

Egypt's landfills are critically overcapacity, especially near large and crowded cities like Cairo and Alexandria. With rapid urbanization and population growth (over 105 million), suitable land for new landfills is rare and competes with agricultural/housing needs. Egypt‟s informal waste sector (the Zabbaleen) plays a major role in waste recovery. In Greater Cairo, they collect up to 60 % of solid municipal waste, with around 50,000 processors and 150,000 collectors, sorters, and transporters. There are 33,000 informal household waste collectors in Cairo alone. Displacement of these workers leads not only to economic hardship for thousands of families but also removes a low-cost, high-productivity sorting network. Some informal systems reportedly recycle up to 80 % of collected waste. Most of Egypt's municipal solid waste (95%) goes to landfills. It‟s a big loss of raw materials (plastic, paper, metals) that could be recycled to produce new products.

Impacts

1.Bad impact on human health:

The health of people who live near waste disposal sites becomes threatened when waste is not disposed of correctly. The staff members who work at landfills face a high risk of health problems. People who handle waste without proper care will develop skin problems and blood infections, breathing difficulties, growth issues, and reproductive health problems. The sewage areas serve as habitats for mosquitoes and rats, which transmit dangerous diseases to humans. The breeding of mosquitoes occurs in water-filled cans and tires, which become their habitat while they spread diseases like malaria and dengue. The landfills and sewage areas provide rats with food and housing while they transmit dangerous diseases, including leptospirosis and salmonellosis. The wet conditions that waste produces create an environment where mold can multiply. The bacteria exist to multiply when they find suitable conditions, which include moisture that comes from appliances and food waste.

2.Economic Inefficiency and High Financial Costs of Fragmented Systems:

The present recycling system creates major financial problems because it operates at a high cost for municipalities to maintain. The absence of a unified national waste management system prevents waste from becoming an economic resource because recycling activities function independently through formal and informal, and private sector programs. The current system creates duplicate infrastructure in particular regions while leaving wide service gaps in other areas. The current recycling system faces major operational challenges because it lacks proper source sorting and collection methods, which leads to the mixing of valuable recyclable materials with large quantities of organic waste that make up most of Egypt's municipal solid waste. The high amount of contamination in waste materials requires additional energy for sorting and cleaning operations, which leads to processing costs rising to unsustainable levels. The unpredictable market prices for recycled materials lead to processing costs exceeding revenue, so recycling operations become expensive municipal responsibilities.

3.Environmental Consequences of Insufficient Infrastructure:

The recycling process collects some waste materials, but open dumpsites and landfills along waterways receive more than 88% of municipal waste. The environmental advantages of dry waste recycling become useless because organic waste makes up 50-60% of total waste and ends up in improper disposal methods. The anaerobic decomposition of organic waste in dumpsites produces methane emissions, which directly cause climate change. The absence of suitable facilities for waste management results in soil and water contamination because leachate seeps into both groundwater and water bodies. The combination of insufficient technology and unskilled personnel at recycling facilities produces substandard products, which need disposal, thus transferring environmental impacts instead of solving them.

This means that each individual in Egypt now has access to much less water than what is required for a comfortable and sustainable life.

Causes

1.Rapid Population Growth:

Egypt‟s population is increasing quickly, which leads to higher demand for water for drinking, farming, and daily use. As the number of people grows, the available water per person decreases. As shown in figure 22, that shows the change in renewable water resources per capita in Egypt from 1962 to 2017, measured in cubic meters per person per year. It is decline over time. However, this amount decreased in the following decades, reaching nearly 1,100 cubic meters in 1987 and falling below 1,000 cubic meters in the early 1990s. After 2000, as water availability dropped to around 800 cubic meters in 2002 and continued to decrease to about 600 cubic meters by 2017. This steady reduction is mainly due to rapid population growth, limited water sources, and increasing demand for water in agriculture, industry, and domestic use.

2.Water Pollution:

Water pollution is another cause of poor water resource. Many industrial factories discharge untreated wastewater into rivers and canals, containing chemicals, heavy metals, and toxic substances. These pollutants contaminate freshwater sources and make them unsuitable for drinking or irrigation. As shown in figure 23, That shows Egypt‟s progress in expanding access to safe drinking water from 1962 to 2017. Initially, the number of people without access rose sharply from 1 million to over 4 million by 1992, as rapid population growth overtook infrastructure development. A period of stability followed until 2000, when major investments likely linked to Millennium Development Goals activated a dramatic decline. By 2015, only about 1.5 million lacked access, reflecting successful policy interventions. However, a slight uptick after 2015 suggests emerging challenges from continued population growth or aging infrastructure. Overall, the chart illustrates Egypt‟s significant long-term progress despite demographic pressures.

3.Limited Water resources:

Egypt‟s water resources are extremely limited, making the efficient use of water essential for sustainable development. The country relies almost entirely on the Nile River, which provides about 98 % of its water supply, and there are very few alternative sources such as rainfall and groundwater. The Nile water share to be paid to Egypt under international agreements is fixed at approximately 55.5 billion m³ per year, and small contributions come from deep aquifers, rainfall, and detoxification plants together these totals around 59 billion m³ annually. Meanwhile, Egypt‟s annual water demand is much higher and per capita availability has declined sharply over the decades. Because Egypt has limited internal renewable water sources and depends on a fixed distribution of externally supplied Nile water, it faces serious pressure to manage this scarce resource more efficiently.

Impacts

1.Less Water Waste:

Improving water use in Egypt has a measurable impact on reducing water waste, even though total water resources remain limited. As shown in figure 24, Egypt‟s total renewable water resources per person have dropped to around 510 m3 per year, reflecting both population growth and demand pressures. This limited supply makes reducing water loss from leaks and inefficient irrigation especially important.

Currently, losses from old and damaged distribution systems and flood irrigation represent significant waste in the system. When authorities repair networks or introduce modern irrigation like drip systems, more of the limited water actually reaches farms and homes, reducing unnecessary waste and improving overall water availability without increasing supply. This means that improved water use contributes directly to more sustainable daily water management in agriculture and urban areas, optimizing the resources that Egypt already has.

2.Better Crop Production:

Agriculture is the largest consumer of water in Egypt, using about 86% of total water withdrawals according to FAO data. This heavy dependence means improving irrigation efficiency is essential for national water security. Most Egyptian farms still rely on surface (flood) irrigation, which covers about 85% of irrigated land and has relatively low efficiency. Studies show that field irrigation efficiency may be only around 50%, meaning large amounts of water are lost through seepage, evaporation, and over-watering. In addition, billions of cubic meters of irrigation water are lost along canals and distribution systems every year. Improving irrigation methods, canal maintenance, and crop selection can significantly reduce these losses and increase water productivity.

3.Irrigation modernization on farmers and water efficiency:

Improving irrigation systems in Egypt has produced measurable benefits in water use and agricultural productivity. As demonstrated in figure 25, Official project results show that rehabilitated irrigation channels increased application efficiency from about 50-60% to 63- 89%, meaning a larger share of water actually reaches crops. Water productivity for wheat nearly doubled, rising from 0.93 kg per cubic meter to 1.75 kg per cubic meter after improvements. These efficiency gains also translated into economic benefits, as World Bank irrigation projects reported a 13- 25% increase in farmers‟ net income. The data demonstrate that better canal design, modern irrigation methods, and improved water management reduce losses, increase crop output per unit of water, and improve rural livelihoods. This confirms that water-use resources improvements can significantly enhance Egypt‟s agricultural sustainability.

In addition, this project only operates on a small scale that suits the small villages or houses, as shown in Figure 26. This project was applied in many regions around the world, such as Nepal, one of the countries that uses micro hydro because of its mountainous nature and the rivers that it has. Mechanism

The system of Microhydropower is turning the power of water into electricity in three main stages, at first, it starts to convert the potential and kinetic energy into mechanical energy. Water has potential energy because of its height and kinetic energy because of its speed. When the water goes into the turbines, it presses on the feathers and then generates torque, and the main column starts to spin. The torque in this case depends on the rate of flow, which is symbolized by (Q), and the difference in height (h). And the main physical equation to calculate the power that is being generated is:

P = 𝜌 × 𝑔 × 𝑄 × ℎ × η

Where: P is the power, 𝜌 is the density of water (1000kg/ m^3), g is the gravity, Q is the flow rate, and η is the system efficiency.

After that, we start with electric conversion. The generator works by converting the motion into electricity. The main idea depends on the electromagnetic induction, which was discovered by Michael Faraday. Simply, when we move a magnet near a copper wire, an electric current is generated in the wire. As shown in Figure 28, the generator has three main parts; they are rotor, the stator, and the magnetic field. The rotor is the spinning part; it‟s an electric magnet and the part connected to the turbine column. When the turbine spins, the rotor spins. And when the rotor spins, the magnetic field is constantly changing. This change cuts through the copper wires, generating an electric voltage. The current then starts to flow through the wire. And this is a direct application of Faraday's law.

The motion is converted into electricity because the electrons inside copper are affected by the changing magnetic field, so they move in a certain direction, and this movement is the electric current. In Microhydropower projects in Nepal, the turbine rotates about 1000 RPM, and the generator produces about 230 volts AC with a capacity of nearly 30 kilowatts.

The Aswan High Dam is one of the most important engineering projects in Egypt. It was built across the Nile River in the 1960s. Before the dam was constructed, the Nile‟s annual floods were random. Some years caused destructive floods, while others led to severe droughts that affected agriculture and water supply. The dam created a lake known as Lake Nasser, which stores water during high-flow seasons and releases it when needed.

One of the main goals of the dam was to produce electricity. The hydroelectric power station inside the dam uses the force of falling water to spin turbines and generate electricity. When it was first completed, the dam supplied a large percentage of Egypt‟s total electricity demand, helping the country develop and update its infrastructure.

In addition to energy production, the dam improved agricultural stability. Farmers could now rely on a consistent water supply instead of depending on unpredictable floods. This allowed for year-round irrigation and increased crop production. The project also provided protection from the disturbing floods that had damaged villages and farmland.

However, despite its many benefits, the dam also introduced several environmental and social challenges. These include changes to natural sediment flow, displacement of communities, and ecological effects downstream. While the Aswan High Dam remains a main national achievement, its limits have encouraged the development of smaller, more flexible hydroelectric solutions such as small hydro plants designed to generate electricity with fewer environmental and social impacts.

Mechanism

The Aswan High Dam operates by converting the potential energy of stored water into electrical energy. It is built across the Nile River and forms a large lake called Lake Nasser behind it. As demonstrated in Figure 30, which shows a mechanism of the high dam for generating electricity. Water stored in the reservoir exerts hydrostatic forces on the dam and has high potential energy. It flows through the floodgate, accelerating due to gravity, and strikes the turbine blades, converting potential energy into mechanical energy. The turbine drives a generator in the powerhouse, producing electricity, which is then stepped up by a transformer for transmission. Draft tubes direct water safely to the downstream tail water. The figure also highlights structural considerations, including hydrostatic uplift, ensuring the dam resists water pressure while efficiently converting water energy into electricity. Each turbine is connected to a generator. When the turbine spins, it turns the generator‟s rotor inside a magnetic field. This movement creates electrical current through electromagnetic induction. The generated electricity is then sent to transformers, which increase the voltage for efficient long-distance transmission. From there, the electricity travels through power lines to cities, factories, and homes across the country.

The dam also includes tubes and control gates that regulate the amount of water released downstream. These systems ensure safe operation during high water levels and help maintain a steady flow for power generation and irrigation. By controlling water storage, flow rate, and turbine speed, the dam produces a consistent supply of renewable electricity while also managing flood control and water distribution.

The Ames Hydroelectric Generating Plant

Overview Mechanism Strength Weakness

A major shift in how electricity comes from flowing water began once the Ames plant started up in 1891, high above Colorado‟s rivers. Built by L.L. Nunn alongside Westinghouse team members, it wasn‟t just another old sawmill turning logs. This time, high- voltage shifting current - AC - powered machinery without cables heats up underground. Back then, fights over direct versus alternating electricity filled newspapers and homes. One camp pushed DC; the other backed AC - tensions ran hot. Yet here, far from cities, water moved a motor far beyond arm‟s length. Power traveled along wires through mountains, valleys, and even states. Distance didn‟t stop it; delivery became its purpose.

High up, near 3,650 meters, stood the Gold King Mine, where money troubles grew like weeds. Hauling coal from far-off peaks had become too expensive to keep operations running. So, Nunn turned his eyes elsewhere to the South Fork of the San Miguel River. It lay about four hundred meters below, just under four kilometers down the mountain. Water rushed through a tight iron pipe under heavy pressure. From the top of the mountain, it flowed downward, powering a 6-foot Pelton Wheel spinning fast because of the strong force behind it. Linked by a belt, that movement turned a 100-horsepower Westinghouse machine into motion.

What made the Ames plan stand out was picking Alternating Current right away. Direct Current - DC - would drop sharply even at brief distances, limiting its reach. In contrast, the AC method used here carried power across those same 4 kilometers with almost no drain. On June 21, 1891, someone threw the switch; the machine underground started vibrating, showing that electricity could tap far-off water to run work far away.

Power transmission stopped being a question after the Ames Plant worked well. Its success shaped what came next, the huge effort at Niagara Falls, and showed how water-based electricity might move people without smoke or noise. Now, where that system once stood, machines still spin power from moving water, quiet proof of old ideas turned real. That place now stands as proof of what happens when water pushes electricity through magnets, shifting energy like an invisible force across great distances.

Mechanism

The mechanism of the Ames Hydroelectric Generating Plant is a masterclass in energy transformation, representing the first time in history that the "potential" of a mountain stream was successfully converted into "industrial motion" kilometers away. As shown in Figure 32. The system functions as a continuous chain of energy conversion, moving from hydraulic pressure to mechanical rotation, and finally to electromagnetic waves by the Pelton Wheel and Westinguge Alternator. This happens in many phases, which are the Hydraulic Phase: Potential to Kinetic Energy, the Mechanical Phase: Kinetic to Rotational Torque, the Electrical Phase: Rotation to Alternating Current, and the Transmission Phase: Voltage Regulation.

Firstly, the process begins by diverting a river into a penstock (a long iron pipe) that drops 300 meters. This significant height uses gravity to build massive pressure as the water falls. As the water reaches the bottom, it is forced through a narrowed channel. As shown in Figure 33, this physical squeeze acts as a catalyst by decreasing the cross-sectional area, using this law makes the velocity increase as there is an inverse relation, converting the heavy potential energy (pressure) into high-velocity kinetic energy (motion). The result is a concentrated, powerful jet of water designed to provide the mechanical force necessary to power the plant.

Secondly, As shown in Figure 34 right in the middle of its setup comes this fast- moving jet - aiming straight at the 6-foot Pelton Wheel. This particular type runs on impulse power, equipped with unique bowl-like buckets shaped around a central splitter piece. When the stream strikes that splitter, movement shifts sideways, water turns sharply, heading right back where it began. Water gives up its greatest momentum when moving backward, thanks to physical rules. That momentum shifts into the wheel. Because of this, the main rod turns with strong force and consistent motion.

Thirdly, from the center of the Pelton Wheel, a fast-moving rod sends power through an enormous leather belt out to a big machine built by Westinghouse. As shown in Figure 35 That device holds moving magnets called the rotor that whirl around while surrounded by fixed copper loops, known as the stator. When those shifting magnets race by, they stir up electric currents in the nearby wires. When the magnets spin, they shove and then drag electron flows in motion, forming a single-phase alternating current.

Fourthly, four kilometers separated the power source from the Gold King Mine, so most of it had to travel as electricity. To cover that distance using less energy, the team chose high-voltage transformers. These transformed incoming power up to 3,000 volts into a stronger electrical signal. That boosted voltage moved easily along the wire lines, losing barely anything along the way. Once at the mine site, another kind of transformer brought those extreme voltages back down. Lower voltage was then reached the motor was safely. Only then did it turn movement from electricity: spinning, turning, breaking rock into smaller pieces.

Points of Strength

1.Superior Transmission Efficiency (AC vs. DC):

What made the Ames plant stand out was choosing Alternating Current. Back then, Direct Current ruled the scene, yet it lost huge amounts of power when transmitted far because wire resistance drained it. Thanks to Westinghouse‟s setup, the facility took advantage of AC that allowed voltages to climb using transformers instead. So electricity could move those four kilometers to the Gold King Mine, losing next to nothing, showing that factories powering tools didn‟t require units right beside them.

2.High-Head Hydraulic Advantage:

Built near the top of a steep slope, the setup leaned on gravity to drive power about 300 meters of vertical force pushed water downward. Efficiency stood out, since a tight, extended penstock turned elevation into pressure. A single Pelton Wheel, just six feet across, responded with sharp rotation when pushed by that flow. From height came motion, then momentum, finally power: around 100 horsepower pulled from speed. Surprisingly, a tiny river trickles, climbing high above huge, slow ones down below.

3.Economic Scalability and Sustainability:

Long before the Ames facility existed, the Gold King Mine teetered on the verge of collapse. Monthly coal transport bills eclipsed $2,500. Power from water turned those expenses into something closer to $500. Such a shift revealed how deeply hydroelectric could cut costs. After constructing the basic setup, dam, pipe, wires, water became the costless driver, shaping how industry could grow without collapse through much of the 1900s.

Points of Weakness

1.High Vulnerability to Seasonal Weather:

The Ames plant depended on water from a high-head source - drawn directly from the South Fork of the San Miguel - so its flow stayed out of human control. Freezing hits hard in Colorado's harshest winters, as water pipes and flumes turn solid, cutting output sharply. Come dry summers, though, levels sink too low, putting high-voltage needs at risk. Coal plants run on constant fuel stockpiles; Ames relies on forces outside its control, rain, cold, ice, all shaping its power rhythm.

2.Extreme Mechanical Stress and Maintenance:

What gave the plant its strength - a strong jet of pressurized water now quietly works against it from within. From up high, muddy water rushed toward the Pelton Wheel, full of grit and chunks. High pressure turned tiny bits like rough sawdust into wearers, scratching away at metal parts fast. Wheel buckets wore thin. Nozzle failed more often than expected. Machines sat idle while fixes took place. Still, the leather belts linking the wheel to the alternator tended to slip or break when torque surged too much, so workers regularly adjusted them by hand.

3.Safety hazards from early high-voltage AC systems:

Back then, in 1891, high-voltage Alternating Current felt like something wild, unpredictable, and almost mysterious. A flaw existed, as no modern circuit breakers or safe grounding systems were in place back then. Those high-voltage lines ran at 3,000 volts, putting laborers and bystanders at serious risk. People actually worried about lightning hitting the wires, fearing damage would spread inside the system. Back then, insulation wasn‟t very advanced, so sparks and hidden currents posed real dangers at the mine site, plus the power facility, too.

Intake Basin in a V shape, coated with zebra-like yellow and black stripes, was selected as the basin stabilizes the turbulent water entering from the waterfall. The concentrator's function is Hydraulic Amplification. In a low-head environment like Wadi El Rayan, capturing a large volume of water and forcing it into a smaller space is the only way to achieve the high-speed flow necessary to drive an impulse turbine. It acts as a passive pump, "charging" the water with kinetic energy before it even enters the pipes. The primary reason for using alternating stripes in flowing water is a phenomenon known as „Disruptive Coloration‟. When water moves rapidly over a striped surface, it creates a visual distortion called „Motion Dazzle‟. This effect overwhelms the fish's optical tracking system, making it nearly impossible for the fish to accurately perceive the object's distance, speed, or dimensions. In a high-flow environment, this uncertainty triggers a natural „avoidance response‟, as the fish perceives the area as unstable or hazardous.

Two penstocks of 600mm diameter were selected as the Penstock acts as the accelerator of the system. By maintaining a constant two 600mm diameter, it delivers a massive flow rate of 1.5m3/s at a calculated velocity of 19m/s directly toward the turbine.

It is the bridge between the hydraulic energy of the waterfall and the mechanical energy of the shaft. Its design allows it to operate at high RPMs even with the relatively lower head of 20 meters, provided the jet is properly aligned with the bucket splitters. The function of the bucket geometry is Energy Harvest Optimization. By precisely controlling the path of every water droplet, the buckets ensure that the water leaves the turbine with almost zero kinetic energy, meaning the maximum possible amount of power has been transferred to the system.

The penstock is made from High-Density Polyethylene (HDPE) Pipe (PN10 Rating) because it is UV- resistant and flexible enough to follow the natural terrain of the Wadi El Rayan cliffs. It handles Water Hammer better than rigid PVC. It is used to transport water from the intake to the turbine with minimal friction loss. The nozzle is made from Hardened Stainless Steel (400 Series) because this is the highest-wear area. Water moves here at 19.8m/s. Hardened steel prevents the sandpaper effect of micro-silt from widening the jet, which would drop efficiency. It converts the high-water pressure to high jet velocity (19.8m/s). The Pelton runner is made from Cast Stainless Steel (Grade 316). Grade 316 offers superior resistance to the slightly saline/alkaline water of the Fayum lakes. It prevents pitting and rust. The water jet hits the center splitter of the bucket and transfers nearly all its momentum to the wheel. The selected gearbox is a Helical Parallel Shaft Gearbox with a gear ratio of approximately 8.6:1. This design is chosen specifically for its high mechanical efficiency, often exceeding 96%, and its ability to handle the high torsional stress generated by a 1-meter diameter wheel. The primary function of this gearbox is speed multiplication. A brushless AC Synchronous Alternator was selected for the electromagnetic conversion of mechanical energy into regulated electrical power.

2.Bell mouth

The bell mouth inlet is a shaped aerodynamic or hydrodynamic inlet designed to guide fluid flow such as air or water into a tube, pipe, or turbine, as shown in Figure 58, with negligible turbulence and energy loss. It‟s a precision tool used to measure flow, test turbine, and hydraulic machinery to produce a uniform, well developed velocity profile at the inlet plane. They are considered the reference standard for inlet flow conditioning.

The bell mouth works by converting static pressure to velocity pressure in a controlled method. As fluid enters the wide inlet and flows toward the narrower throat (exit plane), it accelerates according to the continuity equation.

The curved shape tricks the air and makes it behave completely in order without turbulences or energy loses, furthermore it is engineered to remove boundary layer separation. By maintaining a negative pressure gradient throughout the contraction, the boundary layer remains thin and attached. This produces a highly uniform, flat velocity profile at the exit plane, which is essential for accurate flow measurement and efficient turbine inlet conditions.

While the Bell mouth geometry is superior for optimizing intake flow, its application in the prototype was limited by manufacturing complexity and cost efficiency. Achieving the precise oval profile required high-end 5 Axis machining to prevent flow separation, which would have significantly overestimated the research and development budget for a single component. Therefore, the bell mouth wasn‟t applied in the prototype.

3.Coanda Screen

The Coanda screen is a highly efficient and effective water intake system that uses the Coanda effect to create smooth and efficient water flow, as shown in figure 59. Whether you're looking to extract water for power generation, industrial processes, or municipal water supply, the Coanda screen water intake system is worth considering.

A Coanda screen intake influences the Coanda Effect to provide an efficient and low maintenance solution for capturing water and minimizing fragments and aquatic organisms' intake.

The Coanda effect describes the tendency of a fluid jet to stay attached to a convex surface. When a fluid stream meets a curved surface, it tends to stick to it rather than following a straight path. This principle is widely applied in various engineering fields, including aerodynamics and fluid dynamics.

Water flows over the screen surface at a controlled angle, adhering to the screen due to the Coanda Effect. As the water adheres to the screen, it passes through the fine slots, while fragments, leaves, and other particles are left behind. After that, the clean water is collected on the downstream side of the screen and redirected for use, while debris is either washed away by the current or collected for manual removal.

The screen is engineered in curved shape, typically made of stainless steel, with specifically angled openings to allow water to pass through, preventing fragments from entering.
